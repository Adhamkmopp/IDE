How has the weather changed over the past 100 years?

First Visualization


  • Motivation: The visualisation shows the changes in temperature over the trimesters December-January-February, March-April-May, June-July-August and September-October-November and the annual mean over the years from 1889 to 2017 in the city of Antananarivo in Madagascar.
  • Method (data set description and preparation): The data set comes from the NASA GISS and it was parsed using this script. The years in which the values of the temperatures where not present have been removed. The dataset contains entries in three columns, Year, Month (mean of the trimesters) and temperature registered.
  • Results/discussion: The plot shows a tendency of the first years analysed (in the blue gradient) where temperatures were lower across the trimesters. It is interesting to see that, being Madagascar in the southern emisphere, the coldest temperatures are registered in the June-July-August trimester with values reaching 0Â°C in the very early years.
  • Conclusions: This visualisation is not interactive at this one taken as a reference and the original data contained all the temperatures for all the months but they didn't look esthetically acceptable in the same plot. So instead the means of the trimesters are represented. On top of that another idea of visualisation was to have lines of the years to appear one after the other in the chronological order.

Second Visualization



  • Motivation: This visualization of monthly temperature data of the past 100 years aims to investigate how temperatures have changed over the past century in Berlin
  • Method (data set description and preparation): The data was collected from a metereological station in Berlin (Berlin-Tempel (52.5 N,13.4 E)) by NASA. A few values were missing and were coded as 999.9 or 999.90. These were manually estimated as the average of neighboring months. If neighboring months were also absent, then estimates were obtained from the averages of neighboring months of a previous year. This was sufficient to account for every missing value.
  • Results/discussion: As seen in the plot, there appears to be a general increase in temperatures. This is especially noticable in January, the coldest month of year. The actual increase however might not be large enough to be considered significant.
  • Conclusions: As stated in the discussion there seems to be a general increase in overall temperature, and in turn, that finding could lend support to the theory of global warming. What the visualization alone fails to deliver is a meaningful statistic to test for the significance of such an increase .The data itself may need to be adjusted to match the technological advancements in the accuracy of the equipment used to take the readings, and to exlclude unreliable past readings. It it also important to remember that this was a visualization on temperatures taken in Berlin only, and therefore locally restricted. A broader representation of disparate locations should be considered before arriving to serious conclusions planet-wise
